# De Bono's Six Thinking Hats — Framework Reference

## Origin

Edward de Bono introduced the Six Thinking Hats in his 1985 book *Six Thinking Hats*. De Bono, a Maltese physician, psychologist, and author, coined the term "lateral thinking" and spent decades developing practical tools for better thinking. The Thinking Hats became his most widely adopted framework, used by organizations including IBM, Siemens, NASA, and the United Nations.

The method grew from a simple observation: most arguments are unproductive because participants mix facts, emotions, logic, and creativity in the same breath. One person states a fact, another responds with a feeling, a third raises a risk, and a fourth proposes an alternative — all simultaneously. The result is adversarial debate disguised as discussion.

De Bono proposed a radical alternative: **parallel thinking**. Instead of everyone thinking their own way at the same time, everyone thinks in the **same direction** at the same time, then switches direction together.

## Core Principle: Parallel Thinking vs. Adversarial Thinking

Traditional Western thinking — rooted in the Socratic method — is adversarial. Thesis meets antithesis to produce synthesis. In practice, this means people adopt positions and defend them. The goal becomes winning the argument rather than exploring the subject.

Parallel thinking eliminates this pattern. When everyone wears the same "hat," there are no positions to defend. A participant wearing the Yellow Hat looks for value — even if they personally oppose the idea. A participant wearing the Black Hat identifies risks — even if they personally champion the proposal. The hat overrides personal advocacy.

This produces two critical benefits:

1. **Ego separation** — The thinking is detached from the thinker. Pointing out risks (Black Hat) is a contribution, not an attack. Expressing feelings (Red Hat) is data, not weakness. Generating wild ideas (Green Hat) is expected, not eccentric.

2. **Completeness** — Adversarial thinking explores only the territory that arguers choose to fight over. Parallel thinking systematically covers all directions: facts, feelings, benefits, risks, alternatives, and process. Nothing is skipped because no one is motivated to skip it.

## The Six Hats

### 🔵 Blue Hat — Process Control (The Facilitator)

The Blue Hat is the meta-hat. It thinks about thinking itself. The Blue Hat wearer manages the process: defines the focus, selects the hat sequence, announces transitions, keeps participants on track, and synthesizes results at the end.

The Blue Hat always opens and closes a thinking session. At the start, it asks: "What is the subject? What are we thinking about? What is the goal?" It then selects an appropriate sequence of hats and guides the group through it. Between each hat, the Blue Hat summarizes what emerged before announcing the next transition.

The most important Blue Hat discipline is **enforcing hat boundaries**. When someone drifts into a different thinking mode (e.g., raising risks during a Yellow Hat segment), the Blue Hat gently redirects: "That sounds like Black Hat thinking — let's capture it and come back to it. Right now we're wearing the Yellow Hat." This boundary enforcement is what makes the system work. Without it, participants collapse back into mixed-mode argument.

The Blue Hat does not contribute content. It manages the process, not the substance. If the facilitator wants to contribute a Yellow Hat observation, they should explicitly "put on" the Yellow Hat to do so, making the mode switch visible to everyone.

### ⚪ White Hat — Information and Data

The White Hat is pure information. No opinions, no evaluation, no spin. Under the White Hat, participants present what is known, identify what is unknown, and discuss how to acquire missing information.

White Hat thinking uses a two-tier system. **Known Facts** are verified, documented, and sourced. **Believed Facts** are reasonable assumptions that haven't been formally verified. Separating these tiers is crucial — decisions based on believed facts carry different risk than decisions based on known facts, and the White Hat makes this distinction visible.

Equally important is surfacing **information gaps**. "We don't know X" is a White Hat contribution as valuable as any fact. Identifying what you would need to find out — and how you would find it — is core White Hat work. Many poor decisions trace back to information gaps that nobody explicitly acknowledged.

The White Hat's greatest discipline is resisting interpretation. "Revenue declined 12% in Q3" is White Hat. "Revenue declined 12% in Q3, which is concerning" is not — "concerning" is a Black Hat evaluation smuggled in as data. The difference matters because the group processes pure data differently than pre-interpreted data.

### 🔴 Red Hat — Emotions and Intuition

The Red Hat provides a formal, legitimate channel for emotions, feelings, gut reactions, and intuition. It is the ONE hat where no justification is required or desired.

In most professional settings, emotions are considered illegitimate unless disguised as logic. A manager who says "I feel uneasy about this vendor" is expected to justify the feeling. So they manufacture logical reasons — and the original signal (the unease itself) gets lost in rationalization. The Red Hat eliminates this problem by making feelings first-class data.

Red Hat thinking is deliberately brief — 30 seconds to one minute. De Bono designed it this way because extended emotional exploration collapses into rationalization. The longer you explain a feeling, the more your logical mind takes over and edits the original impulse. Brief bursts capture authentic feeling before the logical mind intervenes.

All feelings are valid under the Red Hat, including contradictory ones. "I feel both excited and scared about this" is a perfectly legitimate Red Hat contribution. Complexity is welcome. Resolution is not required — the Red Hat captures the emotional landscape, not a single unified position.

### 🟡 Yellow Hat — Logical Positive Value

The Yellow Hat looks for value, benefit, and feasibility — but with **logical grounding**. This is not blind optimism or cheerleading. Yellow Hat thinking provides specific reasons why something could work, what benefits it might produce, and what conditions would need to be true for success.

Yellow Hat is often the hardest hat to wear well. Cynicism comes easily; reasoned optimism requires effort. "This could work because..." demands more intellectual work than "This is great!" The Yellow Hat insists on the "because."

The Yellow Hat asks specific questions: What are the benefits? Who gains value? What precedents suggest this can work? What's the best-case scenario — and how realistic is it? What hidden or second-order benefits might emerge? The answers must be grounded in reasoning, not hope.

The Yellow Hat is the essential complement to the Black Hat. Together, they provide a balanced logical assessment — the Black Hat identifies what could go wrong, the Yellow Hat identifies what could go right. Neither is complete without the other. Organizations that over-rely on Black Hat thinking (risk-averse cultures) need explicit Yellow Hat practice to function.

### ⚫ Black Hat — Caution and Critical Judgment

The Black Hat is the most natural thinking mode in Western culture and therefore the one most frequently overused. It represents logical caution — identifying risks, dangers, flaws, and reasons something might not work.

De Bono chose the color black to evoke the robes of a judge — someone who must exercise sober assessment and render judgment based on evidence. The Black Hat's value is in **logical negativity**: not pessimism or emotional resistance, but reasoned identification of what could go wrong.

Good Black Hat thinking is specific. "This won't work" is lazy Black Hat. "This fails under conditions X and Y because of Z" is useful Black Hat. The hat demands specificity — exactly what could go wrong, under what circumstances, with what likelihood, and with what consequences.

The Black Hat's greatest danger is overuse. Because caution feels responsible, Black Hat thinking can dominate sessions and kill ideas before they're explored. The Blue Hat's job is to time-box the Black Hat and ensure it receives no more time than the Yellow Hat. This balance is critical — when Black Hat dominates, organizations become unable to innovate.

### 🟢 Green Hat — Creativity and New Ideas

The Green Hat is the generative hat. It produces new ideas, alternatives, provocations, and lateral connections. De Bono intended Green Hat thinking to be active and effortful, not merely permission to "be creative."

The Green Hat's primary tool is the **Provocative Operation (PO)** — a deliberate, illogical statement used as a stepping stone to new ideas. "PO: customers should pay us NOT to use the product" is absurd on its face, but as a provocation it might lead to insights about premium access, exclusivity, or value framing. The word "PO" signals that what follows should be treated as a movement stimulus, not a proposition to be judged true or false.

Green Hat thinking also employs **random stimulation** — picking an unrelated word, object, or concept and forcing connections. "Random word: hospital. What if our onboarding process worked like a hospital triage?" This technique breaks fixed patterns by forcing the brain into new territory.

The Green Hat requires psychological safety. Participants must feel free to suggest ideas without immediate evaluation. This is why the Black Hat should never immediately follow the Green Hat in a sequence — ideas need Yellow Hat support before they face critical scrutiny. The sequence matters.

Green Hat thinking is also responsible for **alternatives**. Whenever a single solution is assumed, the Green Hat asks: "What are the other ways to achieve this?" Even if the original approach is good, generating alternatives enriches the decision by revealing the option space.

## Hat Sequences

Sequences define the order in which hats are worn for different thinking goals. The Blue Hat selects the appropriate sequence at the start of a session.

### Sequence 1: Initial Exploration

**Use when:** First encounter with a new topic, idea, or proposal.

**Flow:** Blue → White → Red → Yellow → Black → Green → Blue

**Rationale:** Start with data (White) to establish a shared information base. Capture initial gut reactions (Red) before logic kicks in. Explore value (Yellow) before risks (Black) to prevent premature rejection. Generate alternatives (Green) informed by the full picture. Blue synthesizes.

### Sequence 2: Quick Assessment

**Use when:** Fast go/no-go evaluation under time pressure.

**Flow:** Blue → Yellow → Black → Red → Blue

**Rationale:** Compressed four-hat cycle. Opens with value (Yellow) — if no value is found, stop early. Identify risks (Black). Gut-check (Red) to capture any intuitive signal the logic missed. Blue decides. Total time: ~10 minutes.

### Sequence 3: Cautious Evaluation

**Use when:** High-stakes, irreversible, or high-cost decisions.

**Flow:** Blue → White → Yellow → Black → Black → Green → Red → Blue

**Rationale:** Extra Black Hat pass for thorough risk assessment. The second Black Hat pass follows Green to evaluate whether creative solutions adequately address the risks identified in the first pass. Appropriate for trap-door decisions.

### Sequence 4: Creative Ideation

**Use when:** Brainstorming, generating alternatives, exploring possibilities.

**Flow:** Blue → Green → Red → Yellow → Black → Green → Blue

**Rationale:** Leads with creativity (Green) before any evaluation. Red provides emotional sorting of initial ideas. Yellow and Black provide balanced assessment. Second Green pass refines and improves ideas based on the evaluation. The double-Green structure gives creativity both an unconstrained start and an informed refinement stage.

### Sequence 5: Strategic Planning

**Use when:** Long-term goals, roadmaps, multi-quarter planning.

**Flow:** Blue → White → Yellow → Black → Green → Yellow → Black → Blue

**Rationale:** Double Yellow-Black cycle. First pass evaluates the landscape (what's possible, what's risky). Green generates strategic options. Second Yellow-Black pass evaluates the options specifically. This thorough treatment is warranted for decisions with long time horizons.

### Sequence 6: Process Improvement

**Use when:** Retrospectives, workflow optimization, improving existing systems.

**Flow:** Blue → White → White → Yellow → Black → Green → Red → Blue

**Rationale:** Double White Hat — first pass examines internal data (metrics, timelines), second pass captures external perspectives (stakeholders, customers, partners). Yellow identifies what to preserve before Black identifies what to fix. Green proposes improvements. Red captures emotional temperature about changes.

### Sequence 7: Problem Solving

**Use when:** Complex problems requiring full exploration and solution generation.

**Flow:** Blue → White → Green → Red → Yellow → Black → Green → Blue

**Rationale:** Early Green Hat after data gathering encourages creative problem-framing before the solution space narrows. Red provides intuitive filtering. Yellow and Black evaluate systematically. Second Green refines solutions with awareness of risks. Best for multi-faceted problems where the problem definition itself may need creative reframing.

### Sequence 8: Performance Review

**Use when:** Evaluating past performance, project retrospectives, post-mortems.

**Flow:** Blue → Red → White → Yellow → Black → Green → Blue

**Rationale:** Opens with Red Hat to capture how people feel about performance before data is presented. This prevents the data from retroactively rationalizing feelings. White then provides the factual record. Yellow and Black evaluate. Green looks forward to improvement.

## Facilitation Rules

### Starting a Session

1. **Define the focus.** State the subject clearly. "We're thinking about X" is not enough — be specific: "We're deciding whether to launch product X in Q3 at price Y targeting audience Z."
2. **Set time expectations.** Total session duration and approximate time per hat.
3. **Select the sequence.** Choose from the canonical sequences or design a custom one. Explain the choice briefly.
4. **Establish the rules.** Everyone wears the same hat at the same time. No mixing modes. All contributions under a hat are valid within that hat's scope.

### Running the Session

1. **Announce transitions clearly.** "We're now putting on the Yellow Hat. For the next 2 minutes, we're looking for value — what's good about this?"
2. **Enforce boundaries.** When someone drifts, redirect gently: "That sounds like Black Hat thinking. Let's capture it and come back. Right now, Yellow Hat."
3. **Summarize between hats.** Before moving on, quickly summarize what the current hat produced.
4. **Monitor balance.** No hat should consume disproportionate time (especially Black Hat). If a hat runs long, the facilitator calls time.
5. **Invite quiet voices.** Some hats favor loud participants (Green, Black). Explicitly invite others: "Before we move on, any Yellow Hat observations we haven't heard?"

### Closing the Session

1. **Blue Hat synthesis.** Summarize each hat's contribution in 1-2 sentences.
2. **Decisions made.** List any clear decisions that emerged.
3. **Actions identified.** List concrete next steps with owners.
4. **Open items.** List what remains unresolved and what information is still needed.
5. **Emotional check.** Brief final Red Hat pulse: "How does the group feel about where we landed?"

### Timing Guidelines

| Hat | Solo Thinking | Group Session |
|-----|---------------|---------------|
| 🔵 Blue | 1-2 min (transitions), 3-5 min (open/close) | 2-3 min (transitions), 5 min (open/close) |
| ⚪ White | 3-5 min | 5-10 min |
| 🔴 Red | 30 sec - 1 min | 1-2 min |
| 🟡 Yellow | 2-3 min | 3-5 min |
| ⚫ Black | 2-3 min | 3-5 min |
| 🟢 Green | 3-5 min | 5-10 min |

## Common Pitfalls

### 1. Black Hat Dominance

**Problem:** The Black Hat feels "responsible" so it gets the most airtime. Sessions become risk catalogues with no creative or positive counterweight.

**Fix:** Time-box Black Hat strictly. Ensure Yellow Hat gets equal or greater time. The facilitator (Blue Hat) must actively enforce this balance.

### 2. Smuggling Opinions into White Hat

**Problem:** "Revenue declined 12%, which is alarming" — the word "alarming" is a Black Hat judgment disguised as data.

**Fix:** The White Hat presenter strips all evaluative language. Facts only. Adjectives like "concerning," "promising," "alarming," or "impressive" are not White Hat vocabulary.

### 3. Rationalizing Red Hat

**Problem:** "I feel uneasy about this because the numbers don't add up." That's Black Hat thinking using Red Hat as an opening.

**Fix:** Red Hat contributions are ONE sentence with no "because." "I feel uneasy." Period. The reasons (if they exist) belong under a different hat.

### 4. Lazy Green Hat

**Problem:** Green Hat devolves into minor variations on the existing idea rather than genuinely new directions.

**Fix:** Use provocative operations (PO statements) and random stimulation to force lateral jumps. The facilitator should push: "That's an incremental improvement. Can we find something more discontinuous?"

### 5. Skipping the Red Hat

**Problem:** The Red Hat feels "fluffy" or unproductive, so it gets dropped from sessions.

**Fix:** Never skip Red Hat. Emotional data is often the earliest signal of problems or opportunities that logic hasn't yet articulated. The brevity of Red Hat (30 seconds) means there's no excuse to skip it.

### 6. No Facilitator Discipline

**Problem:** Without active Blue Hat facilitation, participants mix hats freely and the session collapses into ordinary argument.

**Fix:** Always designate a facilitator. The facilitator's ONLY job during the session is process management — they do not contribute content unless they explicitly "switch hats" and announce it.

### 7. Using Hats as Specialist Roles

**Problem:** Assigning one person as "the Black Hat person" or "our Green Hat thinker." This turns parallel thinking back into positional thinking.

**Fix:** Everyone wears every hat. The hats are temporary modes, not permanent assignments. Even the most cautious team member practices Yellow Hat. Even the most optimistic practices Black Hat.

## Cultural Notes

### Color Associations

De Bono chose the hat colors for their metaphorical associations in Western culture:

| Color | Intended Association | Cultural Sensitivity |
|-------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Blue | Sky, overview, calm authority | Widely neutral |
| White | Blank paper, neutrality | Some cultures associate white with mourning |
| Red | Fire, warmth, passion | Strong emotional association universally |
| Yellow | Sunshine, optimism | Broadly positive |
| Black | Judge's robes, caution | Can carry racial connotations; Grey alternative available |
| Green | Vegetation, growth, "green light" | Broadly positive |

### The Black/Grey Hat Option

De Bono chose "Black" to evoke judicial robes and the sober severity of a judge's assessment — not any racial association. However, some organizations prefer **"Grey Hat"** as a culturally neutral substitute. The thinking mode is identical; only the label changes.

### Inclusive Language

Effective hat sessions use language that is:
- **Non-gendered** — Avoid "he" defaults, use "they" or role titles
- **Idiom-light** — Avoid culture-specific expressions ("knock it out of the park")
- **Accessible** — This is a practical thinking tool, not an academic exercise
- **Solo-friendly** — The framework works for one person switching modes, not just groups

## Applied Extensions in This Skill

This implementation stays faithful to the core De Bono principle of **one hat at a time**, but extends the practical ways the hats can be used in modern AI workflows:

- **Direct hat use:** a user can activate one hat for a focused pass without running a full facilitated session.
- **Temporary deliberate hat invocation:** an active hat may briefly call another hat when the shift is named explicitly and the return path is also named explicitly.
- **Socratic interrogation by hat lens:** the hats can be used not only to generate statements, but also to generate better questions from within each mode.

These extensions preserve the central value of the method — separation of thinking modes — while making the hats more usable as a portable interactive system.
